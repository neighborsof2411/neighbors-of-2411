### About CEQA & Environmental Review

{{< faq q="What does CEQA stand for?" >}}
California Environmental Quality Act (Public Resources Code §21000 et seq.) — the state's environmental review law requiring agencies to disclose environmental impacts before approving projects.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's an 'Initial Study / Mitigated Negative Declaration' (IS/MND)?" >}}
A streamlined environmental review used when the lead agency initially determines a project will have no significant environmental impacts (with mitigation measures). It's shorter and faster than a full EIR but must still meet CEQA standards.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's an Environmental Impact Report (EIR)?" >}}
A comprehensive environmental document (typically 50–200+ pages) analyzing all potentially significant impacts, exploring alternatives, and proposing mitigation measures. Required when there is substantial evidence a project may have significant environmental effects.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's the 'fair argument' standard?" >}}
Under CEQA, an EIR is required if there is **any reasonable argument** that a project may have significant environmental effects — a lower burden than "preponderance of evidence." Example: if the applicant's own noise model shows 53.6 dBA, only 1.4 dBA below the 55 dBA threshold, there is a fair argument that nighttime noise impacts are significant, even though the modeled level is technically below the threshold.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's the difference between a 'less than significant' impact and a 'significant' impact?" >}}
"Less than significant" means the impact occurs but won't substantially affect the environment (due to low intensity or effective mitigation). "Significant" means the impact will substantially affect the environment and requires an EIR and detailed mitigation.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's a 'mitigation measure'?" >}}
A requirement imposed on a project to reduce impacts to less-than-significant levels (sound walls, truck route restrictions, hours-of-operation limits, air filtration, etc.). If mitigation measures are vague, unenforceable, or rely on future regulatory compliance rather than the applicant's binding commitment, they may not be adequate under CEQA.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Can I demand an EIR at the hearing?" >}}
Yes. You can submit public comment stating that substantial evidence supports a fair argument of significant impacts, and therefore an EIR is required. The Planning Commission cannot ignore a fair argument — they must either prepare an EIR or provide written findings explaining why impacts are not significant.
{{< /faq >}}

### About Your Rights

{{< faq q="Do I have to be a resident to comment?" >}}
No. Anyone with an interest in the project can comment — if you work in the area, send your children to nearby schools, or frequent the neighborhood, you have standing.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Can I form a nonprofit to oppose the project?" >}}
Yes. Neighbors of 2411 is a Nonprofit Unincorporated Association (UNA) organized under California law, requiring no formal registration — just a founding document and agreement among members. A UNA provides anti-SLAPP protection and litigation privilege.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Can the developer sue me for opposing the project?" >}}
It's legally risky for them. California's anti-SLAPP statute (Code of Civil Procedure §425.16) protects people engaging in protected speech (public comments, petitions to government, etc.) from frivolous lawsuits, and statements made in official proceedings are protected by litigation privilege (Civil Code §47(b)). Stick to facts from the record, avoid personal attacks, and your comments are legally protected.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What if someone files a Public Records Act request for our group's emails?" >}}
Our emails (neighborsof2411@gmail.com) are not public records unless they're communications with the City or developer. Internal group discussions are private, but assume any email could be forwarded — don't include sensitive information you wouldn't want made public.
{{< /faq >}}

### About Conditions of Approval

{{< faq q="What are 'Conditions of Approval'?" >}}
Requirements imposed on a project that must be met before and/or during operations — hours-of-operation restrictions, truck routing, noise controls, zero-emission equipment requirements, monitoring, and similar.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="How do I propose a condition?" >}}
Speak at the hearing or submit written comments requesting a specific, measurable condition. Example: *"I urge the Planning Commission to impose the following condition: 'The warehouse shall not operate between 10 PM and 7 AM, and no truck traffic shall occur during those hours.'"* Be specific rather than vague.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Who enforces conditions of approval?" >}}
The City's Code Enforcement Division — but enforcement typically requires someone to file a complaint. Make sure conditions include an independent monitoring/reporting requirement, not just complaint-based enforcement.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Can conditions change after project approval?" >}}
Conditions are set at approval and binding, but applicants can apply for a Conditional Use Permit modification or variance to change them — and the community can oppose those changes.
{{< /faq >}}

### About Air Quality & Health

{{< faq q="What's 'diesel particulate matter' (DPM)?" >}}
Tiny solid particles and liquid droplets emitted from diesel engines. DPM is a known carcinogen and linked to respiratory disease. See our [Health Evidence page](/health-evidence/) for the peer-reviewed research.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's the significance of '9.48 per million' cancer risk?" >}}
Background cancer risk from all air pollution in this area is ~300–400 per million (MATES-V study); the project's added risk is modeled at 9.48 per million against a 10.0 per million significance threshold — barely below it. Any modeling error or real-world variability pushes it over.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Is cancer risk 'de minimis' (too small to worry about)?" >}}
No. A 9.48 per million added risk means approximately 10 additional cancer cases per million people exposed over a lifetime. For a sensitive population (children at Fletcher Elementary), this is significant.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's SCAQMD?" >}}
South Coast Air Quality Management District — the government agency responsible for air quality regulation and permitting in Orange County, and the expert authority on air quality impacts. Their deficiency letter is strong evidence the analysis is inadequate.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's CARB?" >}}
California Air Resources Board — the state agency responsible for air quality statewide, which publishes siting guidance recommending sensitive land uses (schools, homes) be kept well away from high-traffic highways and warehouses.
{{< /faq >}}

### About Noise

{{< faq q="What do the decibel (dB) numbers mean?" >}}
Decibels measure sound intensity on a logarithmic scale — each 10 dB increase is roughly twice as loud to human ears. For reference: whisper ~30 dBA, conversation ~60 dBA, busy traffic ~70 dBA, lawn mower ~90 dBA, chainsaw ~110 dBA. A 1.4 dBA difference (this project's modeled noise margin) is barely perceptible to human ears — and easily erased by real-world variability in equipment, weather, or maintenance.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="Is nighttime noise more regulated than daytime?" >}}
Yes. Most jurisdictions have lower significance thresholds for nighttime noise (e.g., 55 dBA at night vs. 70 dBA during the day), reflecting that sleep disruption is more harmful than daytime activity.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's a 'backup alarm'?" >}}
The loud beeping sound that plays when heavy equipment backs up. Federal regulations require backup alarms on vehicles over 10,000 lbs — typically 75–85 dBA, far louder than the modeled dock noise level of 53.6 dBA.
{{< /faq >}}

### About Traffic

{{< faq q="What's 'Level of Service' (LOS)?" >}}
A measure of traffic congestion at an intersection or roadway, from LOS A (free-flowing) to LOS F (gridlock). LOS E/F is generally considered unacceptable for residential or mixed-use areas.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="What's a 'PCE trip'?" >}}
Passenger Car Equivalent. One truck counts as roughly 2 PCE trips because trucks take up more space and affect traffic flow more than cars — so 810 net new PCE trips represents fewer actual vehicles, but a traffic impact equivalent to 810 cars.
{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq q="If the applicant says the traffic impact is 'unavoidable,' does that mean it's not significant?" >}}
No — an "unavoidable" impact is actually evidence that it's significant. Significant impacts require an EIR and analysis of alternatives; the fact that the applicant can't or won't mitigate it highlights the need for more thorough review, not less.
{{< /faq >}}

## Glossary of Terms

**Administrative Record** — all documents, comments, and decisions related to a project; the official record courts review if a project is challenged in litigation.

**Alternatives Analysis** — a required EIR component exploring alternatives to the proposed project that could achieve project objectives with fewer environmental impacts.

**Appeal** — a request to overturn or modify a decision by the next higher authority.

**Conditional Use Permit (CUP)** — a discretionary permit required for certain land uses that may be compatible with zoning if conditions are imposed.

**Design Review** — architectural review ensuring a project meets aesthetic standards and is compatible with surrounding development.

**Environmental Impact Report (EIR)** — comprehensive environmental analysis required for projects with potentially significant impacts.

**Entitlements** — approvals required from the government (permits, CUPs, variances, etc.).

**Fair Argument Standard** — the CEQA legal standard requiring an EIR if there is any reasonable argument a project may have significant environmental effects.

**General Plan** — a city's long-term vision/policy document guiding future land use, transportation, housing, etc.

**Initial Study (IS)** — first-level environmental review determining if impacts are potentially significant.

**Initial Study/Mitigated Negative Declaration (IS/MND)** — streamlined environmental review concluding impacts are less-than-significant with mitigation; contested in this project.

**Lead Agency** — the government agency responsible for approving the project and preparing environmental review (City of Orange, here).

**Level of Service (LOS)** — measure of traffic congestion (A–F scale).

**Mitigation Measures** — requirements imposed to reduce environmental impacts to less-than-significant levels.

**Negative Declaration** — environmental document concluding a project has no significant impacts.

**Notice of Availability (NOA)** — public notice announcing release of an environmental document and comment period.

**Public Records Act (PRA)** — California Government Code §6250 et seq., providing public access to government records.

**Recusal** — when an official removes themselves from a decision due to conflict of interest or appearance of bias.

**Significant Impact** — an environmental effect that would substantially affect the environment, requiring mitigation and/or an EIR.

**Site Plan Review** — review of a project's site layout, building placement, parking, landscaping, and access.

**Standing** — legal right to participate in a proceeding.

**Substantial Evidence** — evidence a reasonable person would consider sufficient to support a conclusion; used in the fair argument standard.

**Threshold of Significance** — a quantitative or qualitative standard above which an impact is considered significant.

**Variance** — approval allowing a project to deviate from zoning requirements.

**Zoning** — designation of how land can be used; M-2 = Industrial Manufacturing here.

**Air Quality Management District (AQMD)** — regional agency responsible for air quality regulation and permitting.

**Carcinogen** — a substance that causes cancer.

**Cumulative Impacts** — combined effects of this project plus other existing/approved projects in the area.

**Decibel (dB)** — unit measuring sound intensity on a logarithmic scale.

**Diesel Particulate Matter (DPM)** — tiny particles and liquids emitted from diesel engines; a known carcinogen.

**Dispersion Model** — a computer model predicting how air pollution travels and disperses.

**Health Risk Assessment (HRA)** — analysis of how a project's emissions affect human health.

**MATES-V** — SCAQMD's Multiple Air Toxics Exposure Study showing air toxics levels regionally.

**PM2.5, PM10** — particulate matter smaller than 2.5 or 10 micrometers; respirable particles that enter the lungs.

**Sensitive Receptor** — a facility or population group especially susceptible to environmental impacts (schools, daycares, hospitals, elder care, homes).

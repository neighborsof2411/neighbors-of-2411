The debate over the 2411 N. Glassell warehouse often focuses on environmental review procedures, traffic impacts, and noise thresholds. But behind these technical discussions is a fundamental question: **what does a 24/7 diesel warehouse do to children's health?**

The answer is documented in one of the most respected longitudinal health studies in the world: the **USC Children's Health Study (CHS)**, conducted by the Keck School of Medicine at USC. For over 20 years, researchers have followed more than 12,000 Southern California children — including students in communities directly comparable to Orange — tracking how local air pollution affects their respiratory health.

- **School**: Fletcher Elementary (TK–6)
- **Students**: ~497
- **Estimated students with asthma**: ~40 (1 in 12 California children, per CDC data)
- **Distance to warehouse site**: 0.25 miles (quarter-mile)
- **Distance from homes to site**: 54 feet
- **Background diesel particulate matter (MATES-V)**: 300–400 per million (already above regional average)

## What Is the USC Children's Health Study?

The USC Children's Health Study is a longitudinal study that has followed more than 12,000 Southern California schoolchildren from elementary school through adolescence and into adulthood, documenting how local air pollution — from traffic, industry, and other sources — affects lung development, asthma rates, respiratory infections, and long-term health outcomes. Its findings have shaped state and federal air quality standards.

**Why it's relevant to Orange**: the CHS studied children in Orange County and Los Angeles communities directly comparable to Orange — the findings are not theoretical, they're based on our neighbors' children.

## Finding #1: Living Near Busy Roads Increases Asthma Risk in Children

{{< finding-card num="1" icon="🫁" title="Living Near Busy Roads Increases Asthma Risk in Children" >}}
{{< callout type="citation" title="The Research" >}}
McConnell R, Berhane K, Yao T, et al. Traffic, Susceptibility, and Childhood Asthma. *Environmental Health Perspectives*. 2006;114(5):766-772. [doi.org/10.1289/ehp.8594](https://doi.org/10.1289/ehp.8594)
{{< /callout >}}

**What the research shows**: children living within 500 meters (~1,600 feet) of busy roads with heavy vehicle traffic have significantly higher rates of asthma diagnosis and asthma-like symptoms. Risk increases with proximity and with traffic volume/intensity.

**Why this applies to Fletcher Elementary**: the warehouse will generate 810 net new daily truck trips — functionally a "new busy road" that did not previously exist on this block. The nearest homes are 54 feet from the site; Fletcher Elementary is 0.25 miles (1,320 feet) north. While the school is technically beyond the 500-meter high-risk zone, the concentration of diesel truck traffic creates an emissions source comparable to busy roadside proximity.

**What this means**: the addition of 810 daily truck trips near a school creates a documented health risk factor for asthma in enrolled students — not speculation, but peer-reviewed research on Southern California children.
{{< /finding-card >}}

## Finding #2: Diesel Emissions Cause Measurable Lung Damage in Children

{{< finding-card num="2" icon="🫁" title="Diesel Emissions Cause Measurable Lung Damage in Children" >}}
{{< callout type="citation" title="The Research" >}}
Gauderman WJ, McConnell R, Gilliland F, et al. The Effect of Air Pollution on Lung Development from 10 to 18 Years of Age. *New England Journal of Medicine*. 2004;351(11):1057-1067. [doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa040610](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa040610)
{{< /callout >}}

**What the research shows**: children exposed to higher levels of nitrogen dioxide (NO2), acid vapor, and elemental carbon show reduced lung-function growth and poorer lung function in adolescence. These are the exact pollutants emitted by diesel trucks, refrigerated trailers, and industrial equipment operating 24/7.

| Pollutant | Source | Effect on Children's Lungs |
|-----------|--------|--------------------------|
| Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2) | Diesel engine exhaust, truck traffic | Reduced lung-function growth during adolescence |
| Elemental Carbon | Diesel particulates, combustion | Chronic airway inflammation |
| Acid Vapor | Vehicle exhaust, secondary formation | Airway damage and reduced lung capacity |
| Diesel Particulate Matter (DPM) | Diesel engines, trucks, generators | Deep-lung deposits; carcinogenic |

**Why this applies**: the warehouse will operate 24/7, generating 810 daily truck trips, loading dock operations, backup generators, possible refrigerated trailers, and idling engines — each emitting NO2, elemental carbon, and diesel particulates.

**What this means**: children at Fletcher Elementary would be exposed throughout their elementary and middle school years — exactly the 10–18 age window the CHS identified as most vulnerable to pollution-related lung damage. A typical warehouse operates for 50+ years.
{{< /finding-card >}}

## Finding #3: Cleaner Air Improves Lung Development — The Reverse Is Also True

{{< finding-card num="3" icon="📈" title="Cleaner Air Improves Lung Development — The Reverse Is Also True" >}}
{{< callout type="citation" title="The Research" >}}
Avol EL, Gauderman WJ, Tan SM, et al. Respiratory Effects of Relocating to Areas of Differing Air Pollution Levels. *American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine*. 2001;164(11):2067-2072. [doi.org/10.1164/ajrccm.164.11.2102138](https://doi.org/10.1164/ajrccm.164.11.2102138)

Gauderman WJ, Urman R, Avol E, et al. Association of Improved Air Quality with Lung Development in Children. *New England Journal of Medicine*. 2015;372(10):905-913. [doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1414123](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1414123)
{{< /callout >}}

**What the research shows**: children who relocate from higher- to lower-pollution communities show improved lung-function growth — proving the relationship is causal. Over the past 20 years, Los Angeles Basin air pollution has substantially improved due to regulation, cleaner vehicle standards, and industrial controls, and Southern California children's lungs have measurably improved during that same period.

**Why this applies to Fletcher Elementary**: this progress represents decades of public health investment. **The 2411 N. Glassell warehouse reverses this trajectory** by introducing a new, concentrated, 24/7 source of diesel particulate matter in an area that has enjoyed air quality improvement.

**What this means**: after 20 years of gains that have translated into healthier children's lungs, adding a 24/7 diesel warehouse moves the meter backward for Fletcher Elementary students, for the 50+ year life of the building.
{{< /finding-card >}}

## Finding #4: Asthmatic Children Are Especially Vulnerable to Air Pollution

{{< finding-card num="4" icon="❤️" title="Asthmatic Children Are Especially Vulnerable to Air Pollution" >}}
{{< callout type="citation" title="The Research" >}}
McConnell R, Berhane K, Gilliland F, et al. Air Pollution and Bronchitic Symptoms in Southern California Children with Asthma. *Environmental Health Perspectives*. 2003;111(16):1861-1867. [doi.org/10.1289/ehp.6408](https://doi.org/10.1289/ehp.6408)
{{< /callout >}}

**What the research shows**: children already diagnosed with asthma are especially vulnerable to air pollution. On higher-pollution days, asthmatic children experience more bronchitic symptoms, increased medication use, more activity limitations, and more school absences.

**Why this applies to Fletcher Elementary**: at ~497 students, and using CDC data showing 1 in 12 California children have asthma, we estimate ~40 children with asthma-related conditions currently enrolled — the District's most respiratory-vulnerable students, facing cumulative risk from an already-burdened air basin (MATES-V background: 300–400 per million).

**What this means**: a significant number of Fletcher Elementary students already live with asthma. A 24/7 warehouse adding chronic pollution next door creates a predictable health risk for this subpopulation.
{{< /finding-card >}}

## Finding #5: High Pollution Days Increase School Absences in Children

{{< finding-card num="5" icon="🔊" title="High Pollution Days Increase School Absences in Children" >}}
{{< callout type="citation" title="The Research" >}}
Gilliland FD, Avol E, Kinney PL, et al. Air Pollution Exposure, Lung Function, and Bronchial Responsiveness in Southwestern U.S. Children. *Epidemiology*. 2001;12(6):613-623. [doi.org/10.1097/00001648-200111000-00006](https://doi.org/10.1097/00001648-200111000-00006)
{{< /callout >}}

**What the research shows**: on higher air pollution days, more children experience respiratory infections and asthma exacerbations, resulting in more school absences.

**Why this matters for Orange Unified School District**: chronic absenteeism is one of the strongest predictors of academic failure. California's ADA (Average Daily Attendance) funding formula ties per-pupil dollars to attendance — a school with pollution-related absences faces direct revenue impact, on top of the underlying health and equity concerns.

**What this means**: the warehouse is not just a health issue — it's an educational and financial issue for the District.
{{< /finding-card >}}

## The Scientific Consensus: Diesel Pollution Harms Children

| Health Effect | Study | Finding | Applies to This Project? |
|---------------|-------|---------|---------------------------|
| Asthma risk | McConnell et al., 2006 | Children near busy roads have increased asthma risk | Yes — 810 daily truck trips create a "new busy road" |
| Lung damage | Gauderman et al., 2004 | Diesel/NO2/elemental carbon reduce lung function in children | Yes — 24/7 diesel emissions |
| Air quality improvement / reversal | Avol et al., 2001; Gauderman et al., 2015 | Cleaner air improves lung function; reversal harms development | Yes — reverses 20 years of local progress |
| Asthma vulnerability | McConnell et al., 2003 | Asthmatic children especially vulnerable to pollution | Yes — ~40 asthmatic students at Fletcher |
| School absences | Gilliland et al., 2001 | High pollution days increase infections & absences | Yes — chronic absence risk for asthmatics |

The findings are consistent with the National Academy of Sciences, American Academy of Pediatrics, American Lung Association, California Air Resources Board siting guidance, and WHO/UNEP global evidence.

## Why the Current Environmental Analysis Falls Short

The City's Draft Mitigated Negative Declaration includes an air quality analysis (Appendix B: Health Risk Assessment). However, it models **cancer risk only** — not asthma, lung function, or respiratory infections — doesn't reference USC CHS findings, doesn't account for the vulnerable population at a school 0.25 miles away, and uses modeling flagged as deficient by SCAQMD.

A proper health analysis would reference USC CHS and similar peer-reviewed literature, model health outcomes beyond cancer risk (asthma, lung function, absences), establish baseline health metrics at Fletcher Elementary, project health impacts over the warehouse's lifetime, analyze operational alternatives, and recommend ongoing monitoring with adaptive management.

## What This Means for Key Stakeholders

### For Fletcher Elementary District

The District has a duty of care to protect students from known environmental health hazards.

- [ ] Demand an independent health impact analysis using USC CHS findings
- [ ] Require baseline air quality and health monitoring before construction
- [ ] Mandate ongoing, developer-funded health surveillance during operations
- [ ] Support the community's EIR demand
- [ ] Push for health-protective conditions if approved (no 24/7 ops near school, no cold storage, zero-emission equipment, independent monitoring)
- [ ] Consider a board resolution creating a public record of District concern

You have direct standing to submit public comment — health and safety of students is your core jurisdiction.

This warehouse generates 810 daily diesel truck trips 0.25 miles from our school. - [ ] Share USC CHS findings with school administrators

## Bottom Line

The debate over the 2411 N. Glassell warehouse should not be abstract. It is about the health of real children — students at Fletcher Elementary and families in the neighborhood, all of whom deserve clean air. The USC Children's Health Study gives us peer-reviewed, locally grounded evidence: asthma risk increases, lung development is impaired, respiratory infections become more common, and school absences rise near concentrated diesel sources.

**The Planning Commission must**: demand a full Environmental Impact Report examining health impacts on Fletcher Elementary and nearby residents; impose strict conditions if approved (no 24/7 operations, diesel-free equipment, independent health monitoring); and prioritize child health in its decision.
